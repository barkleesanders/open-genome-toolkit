# How to get testing you can actually afford

A directory of free, at-cost, and low-cost ways to get genetic and laboratory
testing in the United States.

**Everything here was verified against the operator's own live page on
2026-07-21.** Prices and programs in this field change constantly and free tools
die regularly, so each entry carries a confidence label. Re-check before you
spend money.

| Label | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **confirmed** | Verified on the operator's own page, or by two independent sources |
| **single-sourced** | One source only — provisional |
| **unverified** | Could not confirm; do not rely on it |

---

## Start here: the decision tree

1. **Do you have insurance?** → several screenings are legally **$0** for you.
   See §1.
2. **Uninsured or underinsured?** → a community health center charges on a
   sliding scale set by your income. See §2.
3. **Just want the numbers without a doctor's visit?** → self-order a panel for
   $33–$59. See §3.
4. **Want genetic testing free?** → join a research program that returns results.
   See §4.
5. **Want your raw DNA to analyze yourself?** → a consumer kit is $29–$99. See §5.

## 1. Free because the law says so

### ACA preventive services — $0, no deductible **[confirmed]**

Marketplace plans and most others must cover these **without any copay or
coinsurance, even before you meet your deductible**, when you use an in-network
provider:

- **Cholesterol screening** (adults at certain ages or higher risk)
- **Type 2 diabetes screening** (adults 40–70 who are overweight or obese)
- **Blood pressure screening**
- **HIV screening** (everyone 15–65, plus others at increased risk) and **PrEP**
- **Hepatitis B** (high-risk groups) and **Hepatitis C** (adults 18–79)
- **Colorectal cancer screening** (adults 45–75)
- Depression screening, alcohol misuse screening, diet counseling, one-time AAA
  screening, fall prevention (65+)

Authoritative list: https://www.healthcare.gov/preventive-care-adults/

> **The catch, in HealthCare.gov's own words: "Coverage may vary. $0 cost isn't
> guaranteed in all cases."** It is in-network only, and the *coding* matters
> enormously — a **screening** lipid panel is free, while the same blood draw
> coded as **diagnostic** (because you have a symptom or an existing diagnosis)
> can be billed to your deductible. When you book, say you want the
> **preventive screening** benefit, and ask how it will be coded.

### Hospital charity care — IRS §501(r) **[confirmed]**

Every **nonprofit hospital** in the US is legally required to have a written
**Financial Assistance Policy (FAP)**. This is not a courtesy; it is a condition
of their tax exemption.

| Requirement | What it gets you |
|---|---|
| §501(r)(4) | A written FAP and a **Plain Language Summary**, publicized |
| §501(r)(5) | If you qualify, you **cannot be charged more than "Amounts Generally Billed"** to insured patients — not the inflated list price |
| §501(r)(6) | They must check your FAP eligibility **before** sending you to collections, suing, or reporting you to credit bureaus |

- https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/financial-assistance-policies-faps
- https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/limitation-on-charges-section-501r5

**How to use it:** call any nonprofit hospital's billing department and ask, by
name, for the **"Financial Assistance Policy and Plain Language Summary."** Many
cover labs entirely at $0 below an income threshold. Most people never ask, and
hospitals are not required to volunteer it.

## 2. Free or sliding-scale by income

### Federally Qualified Health Centers **[confirmed]**

**Find one: https://findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov**

HRSA-funded health centers are legally required to run a **sliding fee discount
program** based on the Federal Poverty Guidelines:

| Household income | What you pay |
|---|---|
| At or below 100% FPG | A **nominal fee** only |
| 101–200% FPG | Partial, sliding discount |
| Above 200% FPG | No discount required |

This covers a clinician visit **and the labs they order** — the practical route
to a lipid panel, CBC, CMP, and HbA1c if you are uninsured. Source:
https://bphc.hrsa.gov/compliance/compliance-manual/chapter9

### Free and charitable clinics **[confirmed live]**

**https://nafcclinics.org/get-care** · phone (703) 647-7427 <!-- pii-scan:allow: public org switchboard, not personal -->

For uninsured and underinsured patients. Eligibility varies by clinic.

### Free infectious disease testing **[confirmed]**

**https://gettested.cdc.gov** — free, fast, confidential HIV, STI, and viral
hepatitis testing, plus HPV and hepatitis vaccines. This covers infectious
disease only, not cholesterol or metabolic panels.

## 3. Order your own labs, no doctor's visit

### First, a correction to how these are usually described

These services are marketed as "no doctor needed." That is not quite true — and
the distinction matters if you want to understand what you're buying. Nearly all
of them route your order through a **contracted physician network** that signs it
on the back end. Labcorp says so plainly: *all OnDemand testing requires an order
from an authorized healthcare professional.*

So the accurate framing is **"no doctor's *visit* needed,"** not "no doctor's
order." That is also why you sometimes see a separate small "physician service
fee" on the bill.

### State restrictions — two widely-repeated claims that are wrong

**"New York bans consumer lab testing."** Overstated. New York's Wadsworth Center
runs a **Direct Access Testing** program that permits labs to offer *certain*
tests directly to consumers without a practitioner order. Everything else needs
an order from a **New York-licensed** practitioner.
https://www.wadsworth.org/regulatory/clep/direct-access-testing

**"Quest doesn't serve AK, AZ, HI, NV, PA, or WA."** Misread. That list is an
exclusion on a **promotional discount**, not on the service. (Separately, Quest's
*in-home mobile collection* is unavailable in AZ, HI, and Puerto Rico.)

**"NJ and RI ban it too."** **Unverified** — no primary source found. Do not
repeat it.

**Practical advice: enter your ZIP at checkout and let the vendor's own
eligibility logic answer.** Any published state list, including this one, ages
badly.

### GoodLabs — free labs for donating blood **[model confirmed, coverage unverified]**

https://goodlabs.com/book-tests/donation

A genuinely interesting model: you **donate blood** at a partner nonprofit blood
center, and GoodLabs runs a clinical lab panel on blood drawn at the same
appointment. The panel is **free with donation**. Lab work runs through
CLIA-certified, CAP-accredited labs (Quest or Labcorp).

**Read the name carefully:** the "donation" means *donating blood*, not
pay-what-you-can pricing. This is not charity care.

**What could not be verified:** their à-la-carte prices, which states they serve,
and the full partner-center list — their site renders entirely in JavaScript and
publishes no server-readable pricing. It also appears to be an early-stage
company. **Use the ZIP-code checker on their site rather than trusting any price
published anywhere else, including here.**

### ❌ QTC is not a route to cheap testing

**This one deserves a direct correction, because it's a common assumption.**

**Leidos QTC Health Services** (qtcm.com) is a **government contractor** that
performs disability and occupational medical exams under contract for the VA,
the military, federal agencies, and insurers. It is one of four VA exam
contractors, alongside Loyal Source, OptumServe, and Veterans Evaluation
Services.

**There is no self-pay door. The public cannot buy testing from QTC.** VA.gov
states it plainly:

> *"You can't start the scheduling process for claim exams yourself. We'll start
> the scheduling process by contacting you."*

https://www.va.gov/disability/va-claim-exam/

**The one real benefit here, and it is a real one:** if you are a **veteran with
an open VA disability claim**, VA schedules your claim exam and any ancillary
testing — X-rays, blood work — **at no cost to you**, with travel reimbursable.
That is a genuine free-testing pathway, but only for veterans in the claims
process, and only for testing VA orders as part of adjudicating that claim.

## 4. Free genetic testing through research

These programs give you clinical-grade genetic results at no cost, because you
are contributing to research. This is the single best value in the whole
directory.

### ⚠️ All of Us Research Program — **no longer returns DNA results**

**https://joinallofus.org** · https://allofus.nih.gov

**This is the correction most other guides have not caught yet.** Nearly every
"free genetic testing" article still recommends *All of Us* for free genetic
results. As of this writing that is wrong. From the program's own
[DNA and Research page](https://www.joinallofus.org/DNA-and-Research), read
live on 2026-07-21:

> *"Between 2020 and 2024, All of Us returned research DNA results to eligible
> participants. **All of Us is no longer returning research DNA results.**"*

Between 2020 and 2024 participants did receive — free — genetic ancestry and
traits, a hereditary disease risk report covering 59 medically actionable genes,
a "Medicine and Your DNA" pharmacogenetics report, and free genetic counseling.
Results went to 220,000+ people. **That program has ended.**

**What this means for you:** joining *All of Us* is still a real contribution to
science, and the program still enrolls. But **do not join expecting to receive
your own genetic results** — that is no longer part of the deal. If your goal is
free results, see the Helix programs below instead.

If you want to contribute anyway — and there are good reasons to, especially
around the ancestry diversity gap described in
[interpreting-results.md §3](interpreting-results.md) — see
[contribute-to-research.md](contribute-to-research.md).

### Helix health-system programs — the most underrated free option **[confirmed]**

If you live in the right region, your health system may offer **free
clinical-grade genetic screening**, no insurance required. In Helix's own words:
*"Because this is a voluntary research program, there is no cost to participate
and health insurance is not required."*

Verified programs:

| Program | Region |
|---|---|
| **Healthy Nevada Project** (Renown) | Nevada |
| **myGenetics** (HealthPartners) | Minnesota / Wisconsin |
| **DNA Answers** (St. Luke's University Health Network) | Pennsylvania / New Jersey |
| **WellSpan** | Pennsylvania |

https://www.helix.com/health-systems/population-genomics-partners

**Check whether your regional health system runs one.** This is often the
cheapest path to clinical-grade screening and almost nobody knows it exists.

### Geisinger MyCode **[confirmed]**

https://www.geisinger.edu/gchs/research/mycode — free DNA sequencing with return
of clinically actionable results. **You must be a Geisinger patient**
(Pennsylvania). 350,000+ enrolled; actionable results returned to 5,000+ people.

### How to find other studies that return results

1. **ResearchMatch** — **https://www.researchmatch.org** — free, NIH-funded
   nonprofit registry connecting volunteers to studies (122,000+ volunteers,
   1,400+ studies, 280 institutions). **The best general-purpose starting point.**
2. **ClinicalTrials.gov** — https://clinicaltrials.gov — filter by *Recruiting* +
   your condition + distance from your ZIP. Read each record's eligibility, and
   check whether individual results are returned — **many studies do not return
   your data to you**.
3. **University registries** — most academic medical centers run a local "join a
   study" registry. Search `"<university name>" research participant registry`.

### ❌ UK Biobank is not an option for you

Frequently suggested, never applicable: it is a **closed UK cohort** that finished
recruiting years ago, and data access is for **approved researchers only**. It is
not a way for a member of the public to get their own results.

It is also, as of **April 2026**, the subject of a major breach — data on ~500,000
participants was found listed for sale on a Chinese e-commerce site, and UK
Biobank temporarily suspended access to its research platform. Reporting indicates
the leak came via academic institutions that had legitimate access.
(Washington Post and three other outlets, April 2026.)

Keep that in mind when weighing *any* "your data will be safely de-identified"
assurance. See [privacy-and-law.md](privacy-and-law.md).

## 5. Consumer DNA kits and raw data

All of these let you **download your raw genotype file**, which is what the
scripts in this repo analyze.

| Service | Price | Raw data | Notes | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **MyHeritage DNA** | **$29** promo (reg. $89) | Yes | Also accepts **free uploads** of other services' raw data for matching | **confirmed** |
| **FamilyTreeDNA** Family Finder | **$79** | Yes | Strong for Y-DNA and mtDNA testing too | **confirmed** |
| **AncestryDNA** | $99 list, often **$39–$59** on sale | Yes | **Largest matching database** — best for finding relatives | single-sourced |
| **23andMe** | see note below | Yes | Product line changed after bankruptcy | **unverified** |
| **Nebula / "DNA Complete"** | historically ~$299 | Yes (WGS: BAM/VCF) | Rebranded to *DNA Complete Inc.*; old product URLs 404 | **unverified** |
| **Dante Labs** | not published | Yes (WGS) | Advertises 30× whole-genome sequencing | **unverified** |

**Buying advice:** if your goal is **finding relatives**, database size decides
everything — AncestryDNA has the largest, and you can then upload that raw file
free to MyHeritage, FamilyTreeDNA, and GEDmatch to fish in their pools too. One
test, four databases.

If your goal is **analysis depth**, whole-genome sequencing gives vastly more data
than any chip — but verify current pricing directly, since none of the WGS
vendors published a server-readable price when this was checked.

### ⚠ The 23andMe story, because it's the cautionary tale of this whole field

23andMe **filed for bankruptcy in 2025**. Regeneron announced a $256M acquisition.
Then a bankruptcy judge instead approved sale to **TTAM Research Institute**, a
**nonprofit led by co-founder Anne Wojcicki**. As of today, `23andme.com`
redirects to `23andme.org`.

**A database containing millions of people's genomes was auctioned in bankruptcy
court.** Multiple state attorneys general objected to DNA being sold as a
bankruptcy asset.

The lesson generalizes to every company here: **your genetic data is a corporate
asset that can change hands when the company fails.** Download your raw data and
keep your own copy.

| Basic bloodwork, uninsured | FQHC sliding scale | **$0 to nominal** |
| Basic bloodwork, no visit | Walk-In Lab lipid panel | **$39** |

| Deep analysis of that raw DNA | This repo's scripts + free imputation + PGS Catalog | **$0** |

**The most common mistake is paying retail.** Before buying any test, check
whether it is a $0 ACA preventive service, whether your regional health system
runs a free Helix program before paying retail for genetic screening.
